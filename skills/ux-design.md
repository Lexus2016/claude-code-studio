Contrarian insight: Most "UX problems" are actually product definition problems.

## UX Research Methods

**Jobs-to-Be-Done Interviews**: Don't ask "what features do you want?" Ask "walk me through the last time you needed to [do this task]." Uncover context, motivations, and workarounds â€” not feature requests.

**Usability Testing**: 5 users reveal 85% of usability problems. Watch, don't guide. When a user struggles, stay quiet â€” the discomfort reveals the design problem.

**Journey Mapping**: Map the full experience from trigger to outcome, including steps outside your product. Identify moments of highest friction and highest emotional impact.

## Key Practices

**Progressive Disclosure**: Show only essential information upfront; reveal complexity as users need it. Don't present all options at once â€” guide users through decisions in sequence.

**Zero-State Design**: Design the first-run experience when there's no data, no history, no context. This is the most critical onboarding moment and the most commonly neglected.

**Error Recovery Design**: Errors are inevitable â€” design them as teaching moments. Show what went wrong, why, and exactly how to fix it. "Something went wrong" is not an error message.

**Desire Paths**: When users consistently do the unexpected (use back button to cancel, click the logo to go home), that's a signal. Design with behavior, not against it.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Designing for Edge Cases First**: Adds complexity that 95% of users never encounter. Clutters the interface and slows development. Solve the core case brilliantly first.

- **Feature Parity with Competitors**: Copying competitors means copying their mistakes. Your product's uniqueness comes from what you choose NOT to do.

- **Skipping Wireframes to High-Fidelity**: Visual polish creates anchoring bias â€” stakeholders and users evaluate aesthetics, not flows. Wireframes keep the conversation on the right problem.

- **Self-Referential Design**: Designing what you find intuitive is designing for yourself. Your mental model is not the user's mental model. Test with strangers.
